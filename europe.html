Tourism in Europe

Where Europe travels

Chapter 1 · Where

Every year Europeans take hundreds of millions of trips inside their own continent. The patterns look chaotic at first glance, but the data tells a much tighter story. First their own backyard, then a handful of neighbors, a handful of countries, a handful of regions. The five views below build that picture, layer by layer.

Origin · Beat 1 / 5

Most tourists are locals.

Color every country by the single nation that sends it the most tourist nights, and the map fills with the countries themselves. For almost every EU member, its own residents are its largest source of tourism. The exceptions are the small, tourism-dependent ones: Croatia is filled by Germans, Cyprus by the British, and Luxembourg by its Dutch and Belgian neighbors.

How to read it

Each country is shaded by its top source of tourist nights in the selected year. Use the dropdown to scrub through years from 2010 onward.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tttr

On that map, almost every country is colored by itself: residents holidaying at home. What happens when we strip out same-country travel?
Foreign origin only · Beat 2 / 5

Strip out the locals, and Germany is everywhere.

Remove same-country travelers and Germany dominates the cross-border map: in 2024 it is the single largest foreign source for twelve of the 27 EU countries, including Austria, Italy, the Netherlands, France, Poland and Croatia. Britain rules the holiday belt (Spain, Greece, Cyprus), and the rest is filled by tight neighbor-pairs: Portugal's biggest foreign source is Spain, the Baltics lean on each other, and the Nordics trade among themselves.

How to read it

Same logic as the previous map, but each country's own residents are excluded before picking the top origin. What's left is the largest foreign source of tourist nights.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tttr, same-country travel removed

Who sends tourists is one story. Now flip the camera and ask the bluntest question of all: which countries actually catch most of these nights?
Trends · Beat 3 / 5

Seven countries soak up most of Europe's nights, and the ranking barely moves.

France and Spain lead by an enormous margin, with Germany and Italy close behind. The 2020 pandemic dip was sharp but uneven: Mediterranean destinations like Spain and Italy lost over 40% of their nights in a single year, while France, cushioned by domestic travel, fell barely 15%. By 2022 the pre-pandemic order was fully restored, with no lasting reshuffle.

How to read it

Annual total nights for each of the seven leading destinations, 2012-2024. Each line tracks one country, labeled directly at its right end. Hover anywhere to compare all destinations for that year.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nim

Those raw totals are honest, and misleading. They reward size. What happens when we account for population?
Geography · Beat 4 / 5

Per capita the board reshuffles, but France and Spain refuse to fall.

Toggle the per-capita switch and the ranking changes, though not the way you'd expect. Croatia rockets to the top, roughly 137 tourist nights per resident, with Finland close behind. But France and Spain barely move: they absorb so much tourism that they stay in the top four even per head. The giants that actually collapse are Germany, Italy and Poland, whose huge totals are spread across huge populations.

How to read it

Total nights per country for the selected year, on a logarithmic color scale so smaller countries don't get washed out. Tick the Per capita box to divide each country's total by its population. Hover for the exact value.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2c

One last zoom. Inside each of those countries, where do tourists actually go? Almost never everywhere.
Regions · Beat 5 / 5

Tourism clusters in regions, not countries.

Zoom inside any country and the same shape reappears: a few hot regions doing most of the work. Veneto and the northern belt carry Italy. Paris, plus the Alpine and Atlantic regions, carry France. The Tyrolean west carries Austria. The rest of each country sits comparatively dark.

How to read it

Total tourist nights per NUTS 2 region for the chosen year, on a logarithmic scale. Pick a country in the left dropdown and the color scale recomputes on its regions alone, so within-country variation stops being washed out by the EU giants.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m, summed over months and residence types

Home first, then a handful of neighbors, countries and regions.

That's the geography of European tourism. But the map of where only tells half the story. The next question zooms into the trip itself: how do they get there, how long do they stay, and where do they sleep? Scroll on.

How Europe travels

Chapter 2 · How

The continental flows have a shape. Now zoom in on the trip itself. How do Europeans get there, how long do they stay, and where do they sleep? Four views that reconstruct the journey.

Transport · Beat 1 / 4

Cars, not planes, move Europe.

Road travel dominates the continent: in 2024 it is the leading mode for 24 of 26 EU countries. Air leads in only two, Cyprus and Malta, both islands where driving across the sea isn't an option. Rail and sea remain a small, stubborn minority everywhere else.

How to read it

Rows are EU countries, columns are years. Each cell is shaded by the dominant transport mode of that country's outbound tourists. Countries are sorted by their most frequent mode, so the bands stack cleanly. The icon at the start of each row is its dominant mode at a glance.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_ttw

They drove in. Now: do they stay for a week, or just a weekend?
Stays · Beat 2 / 4

Almost everywhere, trips run long. The Baltics are the exception.

Across 2012 to 2024, four nights or more was the dominant trip length in nearly every EU destination, Mediterranean beaches and central-European cities alike. The real outlier is the northeast: Estonia is overwhelmingly a short-break destination (one-to-three-night trips won 11 of 13 years), with Finland, Lithuania and Latvia split roughly down the middle. That corner of Europe is built on quick city breaks.

How to read it

A diverging bar centered on 50%. Orange (left) counts years between 2012 and the selected end year where short stays dominated; green (right) counts years where long stays dominated. Scrub the end-year dropdown to accumulate more history.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tnws

Whatever the length of the trip, they all have to sleep somewhere. So where does Europe sleep?
Accommodation · Beat 3 / 4

Hotels just overtook staying with friends and family.

Two types tower over the rest: hotels and free stays with friends or relatives. In 2012 informal stays were the larger of the two; by 2024 hotels had edged ahead to become the single biggest category, near 30% of nights. Camping and own-home stays stay niche but stable. (Eurostat reclassified its rented categories mid-decade, so the drop in "other rented" is partly a definitional change, not a real shift in behavior.)

How to read it

A 100% stacked area chart, normalized so the bands always sum to 100% of nights. Each color is one accommodation type. The labels on the right show the final 2024 share.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tnac

That's the continent-wide picture. Country by country, the story splits in two.
By country · Beat 4 / 4

A clean north-south split: hotels up north, friends and family down south.

Northern, central and eastern countries lean on hotels, 16 of them in 2024. The Mediterranean, plus France, Poland and Finland, lean on non-rented stays: sleeping for free with friends and relatives rather than paying for lodging. Greece stands alone, where travelers most often use their own or family-owned dwellings, a category nobody else tops.

How to read it

Each country is colored by the accommodation type that captured the most tourist nights in the selected year. The legend below the map rebuilds based on which categories actually appear that year.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tnac

End of Chapter 2 · Up next: When

By car, for a long stay, in a hotel or a friend's spare room.

That's the shape of a typical European trip. The next question is timing. Does Europe travel all year long, or only in one big window? Scroll on.

When Europe travels

Chapter 3 · When

European tourism doesn't run on a calendar, it runs on a clock, and the clock has one big hand. The summer months crush every other season almost everywhere. But once you look closely, the picture cracks open: Alpine winters and a scattering of autumn-peaking coasts hide beneath the summer wave.

Country view · Beat 1 / 4

Most countries peak in August, the rest in July.

By 2024 the summer pattern is total: every one of the 26 EU countries records its busiest month in July or August (19 in August, 7 in July). And it is remarkably resilient. Even in 2020, the worst pandemic year, the peak barely moved: 24 countries still topped out in summer, with only Austria and one small island nation slipping to February.

How to read it

Each EU country is colored by the calendar month that recorded the most tourist nights in the selected year. Non-EU countries are shown in light gray as geographic context only.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m

But "the country peaks in August" hides everything interesting. Zoom into the regions and a second story appears.
Region view · Beat 2 / 4

Beneath the summer peak, a small counter-season hides.

At NUTS 2 resolution most regions still peak in summer, 323 of 349 in 2024. But a counter-season pokes through: a handful of Alpine and ski regions peak in February (the Austrian Alps lead them), and a scattering of capital-city regions peak in October, Madrid, Paris and Bucharest among them, when the summer beach rush gives way to autumn city breaks. The country average drowns out everyone who isn't on the beach.

How to read it

The same question as the country map, but at NUTS 2 resolution hundreds of subnational regions each colored by their busiest month. Hover any region to see its name and peak month.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m with NUTS 2 GISCO geometry

Which month a region peaks in is one question. How big the peak actually is that's another.
Intensity · Beat 3 / 4

August doesn't just win, it dwarfs every other month.

Switch to August in any year: the Mediterranean coast and the Alpine arc burn bright on a log scale. Switch to February: most of the continent goes dark and only the ski regions stay lit. The previous map showed which month a region peaks in. This one shows by how much.

How to read it

Pick a month and a year from the dropdowns. Each NUTS 2 region is colored by its total nights for that specific month, on a logarithmic scale so smaller regions remain visible.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m, aggregated across residence and accommodation

So we know the calendar. One last question for this chapter who wins each month of the year?
Ranking · Beat 4 / 4

Summer belongs to Italy, France and Spain. The rest of the year belongs to Spain.

In the peak months the lead trades between Italy, France and Spain: Italy tops June and July, France takes August. But Spain owns the rest of the calendar, ranking first in seven of the twelve months. Germany surfaces only in deep winter, topping November and December.

How to read it

For each calendar month, the bar shows the country that received the most tourist nights in the selected year. Bar length is total nights; color is the country's identifier. Scrub the year dropdown from 2020 to 2024 to watch the seasonal ranking shift.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m

How much it costs

Chapter 4 · How much

The same trip costs wildly different things to wildly different Europeans. The bill depends on who travels, where they go, and how long they stay. The six views below peel those three variables apart and end with the question every traveler eventually faces: short luxury break, or long budget holiday?

Geography · Beat 1 / 6

Small, wealthy and central: that's who spends the most per trip.

Luxembourg, Austria and Germany sit at the top of the per-trip spending map. Their residents take expensive trips on average, partly because they're wealthy, partly because nearly every trip is already an international one. Eastern Europe sits at the other end, with the Czech Republic, Bulgaria and the Baltics spending least, though the gap has narrowed steadily since 2012.

How to read it

EU countries are colored by the average expenditure per trip of their residents. Darker shades mean travelers from that country spend more on average per trip. Scrub the year dropdown to watch the 2020 dip and its recovery.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw

Per-trip totals mix two things: how much you spend each night, and how many nights you stay. Strip out the second one what does a single night actually cost?
Intensity · Beat 2 / 6

Per night, the wealthy and the islanders pay the most.

Switch to per-night spending and the leaderboard changes. Luxembourg, Austria and Ireland pay the most for each night away, with the island nation Cyprus close behind. Eastern Europe pays the least. The trick is the ratio: a country with high per-night and low per-trip takes short luxury breaks; the inverse, high per-trip and low per-night, takes long budget holidays.

How to read it

Same EU countries as the previous map, but colored by what their residents spend per night while traveling. Per-night strips out the effect of trip length, so what's left is closer to taste and wealth.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw and tour_dem_tnw

Until now the maps have asked who pays. Flip the camera around where does that money land?
Destination · Beat 3 / 6

A night abroad has a price tag, and long-haul is dearest.

Now each country is colored by how much European travelers spend per night visiting it. The priciest destinations are nearly all long-haul: the United States and the rest of North America lead, with Asia, China, South Africa and Iceland close behind. Within Europe the dearest are the Alpine and western countries, but they sit well below the long-haul leaders. The reference bars at the bottom give the European average for domestic trips, all foreign trips, and the whole world.

How to read it

Same per-night metric as before, but flipped to the destination side. Eurostat publishes some totals (Africa, Asia, Americas, World) that have no country shape those appear as colored bars at the bottom, on the same color scale.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw_per_dest and tour_dem_tnw_per_dest

Per-night ranks destinations on price. But Europeans don't stay one night they stay longer the further they fly. So what does an entire trip cost?
Destination · Beat 4 / 6

Long-haul trips cost more per night, and far more per trip.

Switch to per-trip and the long-haul destinations explode in color: South Africa and China top the map, followed by South America, Brazil and the United States. The reason is double: Europeans pay more per night there and stay much longer. Neighboring European destinations look cheap by comparison, even where nightly rates aren't far apart.

How to read it

Same world view as the previous map, but showing average spending per trip rather than per night. Aggregate regions appear as bars at the bottom, on the same color scale.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw_per_dest

That double penalty hints at a clean three-way comparison. For each country: a trip at home, a trip elsewhere in the EU, and a trip outside the EU what do they each cost?
Scope · Beat 5 / 6

The further you leave home, the steeper the bill, in every country.

Three bars per country, side by side. In all 26 countries the orange bar (outside EU) is the tallest and the green bar (domestic) is the cheapest, without a single exception. The blue bar (intra-EU) sits in the middle. The gap between green and orange tells you how much extra a true international holiday costs that country's travelers.

How to read it

For each country, three grouped bars show average spending per trip by destination scope: green for domestic, blue for trips within the EU, orange for trips outside the EU. Countries are sorted by their outside-EU spending.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw split by destination scope

One last chart and it puts every country in the same picture. The question we've been circling the entire chapter: does this country take short luxury breaks, or long budget holidays?
Pattern · Beat 6 / 6

Two travel cultures, on one chart.

Each dot is a country. Up and to the right (high per-night, high per-trip) sits the short luxury break crowd. Up and to the left (modest per-night, high per-trip) sits the long budget holiday crowd. The dashed lines mark implied trip lengths of 4, 7, 10 and 14 nights, so each country's average vacation length pops out at a glance.

How to read it

Horizontal axis: average spending per night. Vertical axis: average spending per trip. Each country sits on the line corresponding to its average trip length. Hover any dot for exact figures.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw and tour_dem_tnw
