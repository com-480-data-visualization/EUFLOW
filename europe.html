Where Europe travels

Chapter 1 · Where

Every year Europeans take hundreds of millions of trips inside their own continent. The patterns look chaotic at first glance, but the data tells a much tighter story. A handful of neighbors. A handful of countries. A handful of regions. The five views below build that picture, layer by layer.

Origin · Beat 1 / 5

Most tourists don't come from far away.

Color every EU country by the single nation that sends it the most tourists, and the map fills with neighbors. Germany covers central Europe. Spain claims Portugal. France stretches across the western Mediterranean. The further away a country sits, the rarer it appears on this map.

How to read it

Each country is shaded by its top source of tourist nights in the selected year. Use the dropdown to scrub through years from 2010 onward.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tttr

But on that map, most countries are colored by themselves residents holidaying at home. What happens when we strip out same-country travel?
Foreign origin only · Beat 2 / 5

Strip out the locals, and Germany sends almost everyone.

Remove same-country travelers and the cross-border picture sharpens. Germany becomes the top foreign source for Austria, Italy, the Netherlands and France. Spain's biggest international visitors are the British. Portugal's pull comes from the Americas. The previous map was honest, but it hid this cross-border story under a thick layer of domestic tourism.

How to read it

Same logic as the previous map, but each country's own residents are excluded before picking the top origin. What's left is the largest foreign source of tourist nights.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tttr, same-country travel removed

Who sends tourists is one story. Now flip the camera and ask the bluntest question of all: which countries actually catch most of these nights?
Trends · Beat 3 / 5

Seven countries soak up most of Europe's nights, and the ranking barely moves.

France, Spain and Italy lead by an enormous margin. The 2020 pandemic dip is sharp and uniform across every leader. By 2022 the ranking is back to where it was, with no real reshuffling between countries.

How to read it

Annual total nights for each of the seven leading destinations, 2012-2024. Each line tracks one country, labeled directly at its right end. Hover anywhere to compare all destinations for that year.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nim

Those raw totals are honest, and misleading. They reward size. What happens when we account for population?
Geography · Beat 4 / 5

Per capita, the tourism giants are tiny.

Toggle the per-capita switch and the ranking inverts. Croatia, Cyprus and Malta jump to the top of the scale: they carry more tourist nights per resident than anywhere else in Europe. France and Spain, which tower above everyone in absolute totals, drop into the middle of the pack.

How to read it

Total nights per country for the selected year, on a logarithmic color scale so smaller countries don't get washed out. Tick the Per capita box to divide each country's total by its population. Hover for the exact value.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2c

One last zoom. Inside each of those countries, where do tourists actually go? Almost never everywhere.
Regions · Beat 5 / 5

Tourism clusters in regions, not countries.

Zoom inside any country and the same shape reappears: a few hot regions doing most of the work. Veneto and Lombardy carry Italy. The south carries France. The Alpine arc carries Austria. The rest of each country sits comparatively dark.

How to read it

Total tourist nights per NUTS 2 region for the chosen year, on a logarithmic scale. Pick a country in the left dropdown and the color scale recomputes on its regions alone, so within-country variation stops being washed out by the EU giants.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m, summed over months and residence types

That's the geography of European tourism. But the map of where only tells half the story. The next question zooms into the trip itself: how do they get there, how long do they stay, and where do they sleep? Scroll on.

How Europe travels

Chapter 2 · How

The continental flows have a shape. Now zoom in on the trip itself. How do Europeans get there, how long do they stay, and where do they sleep? Four views that reconstruct the journey.

Transport · Beat 1 / 4

Cars, not planes, move Europe.

Road travel dominates almost every country on the continent. Air takes over only when geography forces it: islands, far edges, anywhere a drive is impractical. Rail and sea remain a small, stubborn minority everywhere else.

How to read it

Rows are EU countries, columns are years. Each cell is shaded by the dominant transport mode of that country's outbound tourists. Countries are sorted by their most frequent mode, so the bands stack cleanly. The icon at the start of each row is its dominant mode at a glance.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_ttw

They drove in. Now: do they stay for a week, or just a weekend?
Stays · Beat 2 / 4

The Mediterranean stays a week. The cities stay a weekend.

A clean split runs across the continent. Beach and island destinations dominate long stays of four nights or more. City and central-European destinations dominate short breaks of one to three nights. The geography of holidays is the geography of how long people sit still.

How to read it

A diverging bar centered on 50%. Orange (left) counts years between 2012 and the selected end year where short stays dominated; green (right) counts years where long stays dominated. Scrub the end-year dropdown to accumulate more history.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tnws

Whatever the length of the trip, they all have to sleep somewhere. So where does Europe sleep?
Accommodation · Beat 3 / 4

Hotels still win but just barely.

Hotels and rented apartments together cover the lion's share of nights. But since 2012 the mix has been quietly shifting: short-term rentals are eating steadily into the hotel slice. Camping and own-property stays remain niche but stable.

How to read it

A 100% stacked area chart, normalized so the bands always sum to 100% of nights. Each color is one accommodation type. The labels on the right show the final 2024 share.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tnac

That's the continent-wide picture. Country by country, the story splits in two.
By country · Beat 4 / 4

A clean north-south split and Greece sleeps with family.

Northern, central and Alpine countries lean on hotels. Mediterranean countries lean on rented apartments vacation rentals, holiday flats, short-term lets. Greece is the only country where residents sleep predominantly in family-owned dwellings, a category nobody else relies on.

How to read it

Each country is colored by the accommodation type that captured the most tourist nights in the selected year. The legend below the map rebuilds based on which categories actually appear that year.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_tnac

End of Chapter 2 · Up next: When

By car, for a week, in a hotel.

That's the shape of a typical European trip. The next question is timing. Does Europe travel all year long, or only in one big window? Scroll on.

When Europe travels

Chapter 3 · When

European tourism doesn't run on a calendar it runs on a clock, and the clock has one big hand. The summer months crush every other season almost everywhere. But once you look closely, the picture cracks open: Alpine winters, Mediterranean shoulder seasons, and one year that broke the pattern entirely.

Country view · Beat 1 / 4

In a normal year, almost everyone peaks in August.

By 2024 the summer pattern is overwhelming nearly every EU country records its single busiest month in July or August. Switch the year to 2020 and the map shatters: countries peak in February, October, December whichever brief windows the pandemic restrictions left open.

How to read it

Each EU country is colored by the calendar month that recorded the most tourist nights in the selected year. Non-EU countries are shown in light gray as geographic context only.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m

But "the country peaks in August" hides everything interesting. Zoom into the regions and a second story appears.
Region view · Beat 2 / 4

Inside every country, a counter-season hides.

At NUTS 2 resolution, the summer-only story breaks. Alpine and ski regions peak in February. A scattering of Mediterranean coastlines and city regions peak in September or October, when prices drop and crowds thin. The country average drowns out everyone who isn't on the beach.

How to read it

The same question as the country map, but at NUTS 2 resolution hundreds of subnational regions each colored by their busiest month. Hover any region to see its name and peak month.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m with NUTS 2 GISCO geometry

Which month a region peaks in is one question. How big the peak actually is that's another.
Intensity · Beat 3 / 4

August doesn't just win it dwarfs every other month.

Switch to August in any year: the Mediterranean coast and the Alpine arc burn bright on a log scale. Switch to February: most of the continent goes dark and only the ski regions stay lit. The previous map showed which month a region peaks in. This one shows by how much.

How to read it

Pick a month and a year from the dropdowns. Each NUTS 2 region is colored by its total nights for that specific month, on a logarithmic scale so smaller regions remain visible.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m, aggregated across residence and accommodation

So we know the calendar. One last question for this chapter who wins each month of the year?
Ranking · Beat 4 / 4

Summer belongs to France, Spain and Italy. The rest of the year is a quieter contest.

Every summer month, the same three names sit on top and by an order of magnitude. The shoulder months and winter shuffle among Germany, France and Spain, but with totals that are a fraction of August's. The calendar isn't just busier in summer; the leaderboard reshuffles around it.

How to read it

For each calendar month, the bar shows the country that received the most tourist nights in the selected year. Bar length is total nights; color is the country's identifier. Scrub the year dropdown from 2020 to 2024 to watch the seasonal ranking shift.

Source: Eurostat tour_occ_nin2m

How much it costs

Chapter 4 · How much

The same trip costs wildly different things to wildly different Europeans. The bill depends on who travels, where they go, and how long they stay. The six views below peel those three variables apart and end with the question every traveler eventually faces: short luxury break, or long budget holiday?

Geography · Beat 1 / 6

Small, wealthy and central that's who spends the most per trip.

Luxembourg, Belgium and Austria sit at the top of the per-trip spending map. Their residents take expensive trips on average partly because they're wealthy, partly because nearly every trip is already an international one. Eastern Europe sits at the other end, though the gap has narrowed steadily since 2012.

How to read it

EU countries are colored by the average expenditure per trip of their residents. Darker shades mean travelers from that country spend more on average per trip. Scrub the year dropdown to watch the 2020 dip and its recovery.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw

Per-trip totals mix two things: how much you spend each night, and how many nights you stay. Strip out the second one what does a single night actually cost?
Intensity · Beat 2 / 6

Per night, the rich pay more and the south stretches it thinner.

Switch to per-night spending and the gap sharpens. Luxembourg, Denmark and Belgium pay the most for each night abroad. Eastern Europe pays the least. The trick is the ratio: a country with high per-night and low per-trip takes short luxury breaks; the inverse high per-trip, low per-night takes long budget holidays.

How to read it

Same EU countries as the previous map, but colored by what their residents spend per night while traveling. Per-night strips out the effect of trip length, so what's left is closer to taste and wealth.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw and tour_dem_tnw

Until now the maps have asked who pays. Flip the camera around where does that money land?
Destination · Beat 3 / 6

A night abroad has a price tag and it varies dramatically by where you sleep.

Now each country is colored by how much European travelers spend per night visiting it. The expensive destinations cluster where you'd expect Western Europe, North America, parts of Asia. The reference bars at the bottom give the European average for domestic trips, all foreign trips, and the whole world.

How to read it

Same per-night metric as before, but flipped to the destination side. Eurostat publishes some totals (Africa, Asia, Americas, World) that have no country shape those appear as colored bars at the bottom, on the same color scale.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw_per_dest and tour_dem_tnw_per_dest

Per-night ranks destinations on price. But Europeans don't stay one night they stay longer the further they fly. So what does an entire trip cost?
Destination · Beat 4 / 6

Long-haul destinations don't just cost more per night they cost more per trip, twice over.

Switch to per-trip and North America and Asia explode in color. The reason isn't only nightly price: Europeans stay there much longer. Per-night cost multiplied by trip length gives long-haul a double penalty. Neighboring European destinations look cheap by comparison even when nightly rates aren't far apart.

How to read it

Same world view as the previous map, but showing average spending per trip rather than per night. Aggregate regions appear as bars at the bottom, on the same color scale.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw_per_dest

That double penalty hints at a clean three-way comparison. For each country: a trip at home, a trip elsewhere in the EU, and a trip outside the EU what do they each cost?
Scope · Beat 5 / 6

The further you leave home, the steeper the bill almost everywhere.

Three bars per country, side by side. The orange bar (outside EU) is the tallest almost everywhere. The blue bar (intra-EU) sits in the middle. The green bar (domestic) is the cheapest. The gap between green and orange tells you how much extra a true international holiday costs that country's travelers.

How to read it

For each country, three grouped bars show average spending per trip by destination scope: green for domestic, blue for trips within the EU, orange for trips outside the EU. Countries are sorted by their outside-EU spending.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw split by destination scope

One last chart and it puts every country in the same picture. The question we've been circling the entire chapter: does this country take short luxury breaks, or long budget holidays?
Pattern · Beat 6 / 6

Two travel cultures, on one chart.

Each dot is a country. Up and to the right high per-night, high per-trip sits the short luxury break crowd. Up and to the left modest per-night, high per-trip sits the long budget holiday crowd. The dashed lines mark implied trip lengths of 4, 7, 10 and 14 nights, so each country's average vacation length pops out at a glance.

How to read it

Horizontal axis: average spending per night. Vertical axis: average spending per trip. Each country sits on the line corresponding to its average trip length. Hover any dot for exact figures.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw and tour_dem_tnw
