Tourism in Switzerland
A closer look at tourism patterns and what drives visitors to Switzerland
Chapter 1 · The Pattern

Every year, millions of Europeans choose Switzerland as their destination. But behind this flow of visitors lies a pattern — one that becomes clearer when you look at the data.

Part 1 · Where

The first question is simple: once tourists arrive in Switzerland, where do they go? The answer is striking. Visitor numbers are heavily concentrated — Zurich, Bern, Graubünden, Valais, and Geneva together absorb the vast majority of arrivals. Most other cantons receive a fraction of that. Switzerland may be small, but not all of it is equally discovered.

Part 2 · Distance

This raises a natural follow-up: where are these visitors coming from? The scatter plot makes the answer immediately visible. Distance is the dominant force. Countries closer to Switzerland send far more visitors — Germany alone accounts for over 1.5 million arrivals. Beyond 1,500 km, the numbers drop close to zero. Geography, it seems, is destiny. Or almost. The United Kingdom sits over 1,000 km away, yet consistently sends hundreds of thousands of visitors — a reminder that cultural and economic ties can bend the rules of distance.

2

Geographic Proximity

Does distance matter?

Key insight

Closer means more visitors

A clear negative correlation exists between distance and arrivals — countries closer to Switzerland consistently send more tourists.

Dominant neighbor

Germany stands out dramatically, sending over 1.5M visitors despite being only ~300 km away. France follows as the second largest source market.

Distance outlier

The United Kingdom breaks the trend — despite being ~1000 km away, it sends a disproportionately large number of visitors, suggesting cultural and economic ties outweigh distance.

Beyond 1500 km

Countries further than 1500 km contribute very few arrivals, clustering near zero regardless of size or wealth.

Source: Eurostat · Distance from Bern · Arrivals data

Part 3 · Wealth

Distance, it turns out, is a powerful predictor. Countries closer to Switzerland consistently send more visitors, with Germany towering above all others. Yet distance alone doesn't explain everything — the United Kingdom, over 1,000 km away, punches well above its weight, hinting that cultural and economic ties matter too. So if proximity shapes tourism flows, what role does wealth play?

End of the Switzerland story

Switzerland's appeal endures — not because of wealth or marketing, but because of proximity, familiarity, and habit. The data confirms what travelers already know: the closer you are, the more likely you are to come. And once the pattern is established, it tends to hold.
