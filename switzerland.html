Tourism in Switzerland
A closer look at tourism patterns and what drives visitors to Switzerland
1

Where in Switzerland

Chapter 1 · Where

Switzerland is small, but tourism inside it is uneven. The same five or six cantons absorb the bulk of every year's arrivals, and the rest sit in their shadow. The first chapter peels that geography apart in three layers: how many visitors land in each canton, which foreign country sends them, and how much of the traffic is actually Swiss residents on holiday at home.

Canton volumes · Beat 1 / 3

A handful of cantons absorb almost everyone.

Zurich leads by a wide margin, followed by Bern, Graubünden, Valais and Geneva. Together these five carry the lion's share of all international arrivals. The remaining 21 cantons split what's left.

How to read it

Each canton is shaded by total EU visitor arrivals in the selected year. Scrub the dropdown to walk through 2005-2025.

Source: FSO · Visitor arrivals by canton

We know which cantons fill up. But who actually fills them - Swiss residents on holiday at home, or visitors from abroad?
Mix · Beat 2 / 3

Geneva is a foreigner's playground. Jura is Switzerland's.

Cantons split sharply: Geneva is over 70% foreign visitors, while Jura is roughly 87% Swiss residents. Big alpine cantons like Graubünden and Valais sit close to half-and-half - they're both international resorts and Swiss favorites.

How to read it

Each row is a canton. Bars sum to 100% of overnight stays - orange for Swiss residents, blue for foreign visitors. Cantons are sorted by foreign share descending.

Source: FSO · Overnight stays by canton and visitor's country of residence

When the visitors are foreign - which country sends them?
Top foreign origin · Beat 3 / 3

Americans top most cantons. Germans hold the Alps and the German-speaking cantons.

In 2024, the largest single foreign source is the United States for Zurich, Bern, Lucerne, Valais and Geneva - a recent shift driven by long-haul recovery. Germany still leads Graubünden, Ticino and Basel. France dominates Vaud, on Lake Geneva.

How to read it

Source: FSO · Overnight stays by canton and visitor's country of residence

End of Chapter 1 · Up next: When

A few cantons. A few origins. Two very different visitor mixes.

We know the geography. But Swiss tourism has a sharper edge than the maps suggest: a clock. The next chapter asks when each canton actually fills up.

2

When Switzerland fills up

Chapter 2 · When

Swiss tourism runs on a strong seasonal rhythm. Most cantons see their busiest month in summer, when arrivals across all sources peak. But the calendar varies canton by canton - and the intensity of the peak varies more.

Top month · Beat 1 / 2

Summer wins almost everywhere. Zurich is the odd one out.

In 2024, most cantons recorded their single busiest month in July or August. Zurich peaks earlier - in June - driven by business travel and conferences before the holiday rush. Scrub back to 2020 and the map shatters: the pandemic forced October and December peaks where summer windows were closed.

How to read it

Each canton is colored by the calendar month with the highest arrivals in the selected year. The legend rebuilds each year, showing only months that actually win somewhere.

Source: FSO · Monthly arrivals by canton

Knowing the peak month is one question. How dramatic the peak actually is - that's another.
Intensity · Beat 2 / 2

Alpine cantons burn bright twice. Urban cantons stay steady.

On a log color scale, two seasonal personalities emerge. Alpine cantons (Graubünden, Valais, Bern, Ticino) show double peaks in February (ski) and August (hiking). Urban cantons (Zurich, Geneva, Basel) have flatter year-round patterns with a single mid-year bump.

How to read it

Rows are cantons sorted by peak month + volume. Columns are months Jan-Dec. Cell color shows arrivals on a logarithmic scale - darker = more.

Source: FSO · Monthly arrivals by canton

End of Chapter 2 · Up next: How

Summer dominates. Winter has a stake. Cities don't follow either.

We've answered where and when. What's still hidden is the shape of the trip itself: how long visitors stay, and whether the pandemic permanently changed who comes back.

3

How visitors stay, and who came back

Chapter 3 · How

Volume tells one story. Behavior tells another. This chapter zooms in on two behavioral signals: how long visitors actually stay per arrival, and how each canton has recovered (or not) since the pandemic broke 2020.

Stay length · Beat 1 / 2

Mountains hold visitors. Transit cantons let them go.

In 2024 the longest average stay was in Graubünden, 2.6 nights per arrival - the canton of Davos, St. Moritz and the Engadin. The shortest was in Schaffhausen and Fribourg, around 1.6 nights, both transit cantons whose visitors typically come for a day-and-a-half then move on.

How to read it

Each canton is colored by overnight stays divided by arrivals - the average number of nights per visitor. Darker = longer stays.

Source: FSO · Arrivals and overnight stays by canton

Stay length is steady. But arrivals were not. 2020 broke them - and the recovery is uneven.
Recovery · Beat 2 / 2

Cities overshot. A few small alpine cantons never came back.

From 2019 to 2024, Geneva, Aargau and Neuchâtel grew arrivals by roughly +20% - they overshot their pre-pandemic peak. At the other end, Obwalden, Uri, Zug and Nidwalden lost 12-21% of their arrivals and haven't returned. The pandemic permanently reshuffled which Switzerland visitors choose.

How to read it

Each line is one canton - left point is 2019 arrivals, right point is 2024. Green = grew, red = shrank. The label shows the percentage change.

Source: FSO · Annual arrivals by canton

End of Chapter 3 · Up next: How much

The trip has a shape. Now: how much does it cost?

Visitors stay 1.6 to 2.6 nights. Cantons recovered or stagnated. The question we haven't asked yet is the bluntest one: how much do they spend per night while they're here, and which origin pays the most?

4

How much they spend in Switzerland

Chapter 4 · How much

Switzerland is famously expensive. But the bill varies dramatically depending on where the visitor comes from - partly taste, partly trip length.

Per night · Beat 1 / 2

Austrians pay the most per night. The Dutch pay the least.

In 2024, Austrian visitors spent roughly €204 per night in Switzerland - the highest per-night spend of any reported origin. Luxembourgers (€201) and Germans (€178) followed close behind. At the bottom: Portuguese (€76) and Dutch (€94). Only 9 EU countries publish this metric for trips to Switzerland.

How to read it

Each origin country is colored by what its residents spend per night while visiting Switzerland. Countries shown in dark gray do not publish a CH-specific figure.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw, filtered to c_dest = CH

Per night is one dimension. The full bill depends on how long they stay too.
Pattern · Beat 2 / 2

Two visitor cultures: long-haul splurge, neighbor short break.

Map the same data onto a scatter. Luxembourgers spend the most per trip (€1,037) - high nightly rate × long stays. Austrians spend big per night but take short trips. Hungarians and Portuguese sit in the budget corner. The diagonal lines show implied stay lengths - most visitors fall between 3 and 7 nights.

How to read it

Horizontal axis: average spending per night. Vertical axis: average spending per trip. Each dot is a source country. The dashed lines mark constant trip lengths (3, 5, 7, 10 nights). Hover any dot for the implied stay length.

Source: Eurostat tour_dem_extotw, filtered to c_dest = CH

End of Chapter 4 · Up next: Why

Spending tells you taste. What determines who shows up?

We've answered where, when, how, and how much. The final chapter takes a different angle: what actually predicts whether a country sends visitors to Switzerland in the first place? Two hypotheses face each other: distance and wealth.

5

Why they come: distance vs wealth

Chapter 5 · Why · Part 1: Distance

The scatter makes the answer immediately visible. Distance is the dominant force. Countries closer to Switzerland send far more visitors - Germany alone accounts for over 1.5 million arrivals. Beyond 1,500 km, the numbers drop close to zero. Geography, it seems, is destiny. Or almost. The United Kingdom sits over 1,000 km away, yet consistently sends hundreds of thousands of visitors, a reminder that cultural and economic ties can bend the rules.

Distance

Closer means more visitors.

A clear negative correlation exists between distance and arrivals - countries closer to Switzerland consistently send more tourists. Germany stands out dramatically (~300 km, 1.5M+ visitors). The UK breaks the trend, suggesting cultural and economic ties outweigh distance.

Source: Eurostat · Distance from Bern · Arrivals data

Distance is a powerful predictor. But wealth might matter too - let's see if it does.

End of the Switzerland story

Proximity, familiarity, habit.

Switzerland's appeal endures - not because of wealth or marketing, but because of geography and the established habits of a few neighboring populations. The data confirms what travelers already know: the closer you are, the more likely you are to come. And once the pattern is established, it tends to hold.
